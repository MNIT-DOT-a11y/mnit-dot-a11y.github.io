Use of Color

Use of color to convey information: WCAG requires that we not use color alone (or visual characteristics) to convey information. Examples are when a chart uses color alone or when a site uses color-coded text to convey information. This is important for users who have any amount of color deficiency, or color blindness.

Informative Languag - Best Practices

State the Message Clearly: Write your message so that the action or alert is obvious even if color is ignored. For example, instead of just coloring a warning yellow, use text like:

“Warning! Your password will expire in 3 days.”

Provide Specific Instructions: If something needs user attention, pair the color or icon with a descriptive phrase:

“Fields with an asterisk (*) are required.” Or “Error: Please enter a valid email address.”

Avoid Jargon or Vague Language: Choose simple, direct words and phrases. Avoid saying only “see highlighted area,” as highlights may not be visible to all. Specify issues, like:

“Please fill out your phone number in the required format: (XXX) XXX-XXXX.”

Combine Text with Other Cues: If you do use color or an icon (such as a red border, or an error symbol), always provide a text explanation close by, such as

“Error: The email address you entered is not valid.”

Use Headings or Labels: Help users navigate and focus with clear section headings or field labels, ensuring important instructions are not missed.

Recap

Use of Color (WCAG 1.4.1):

Recap: Requires that color is not the only visual means of conveying information, indicating an action, or prompting a response.

Best Practices for testing:

  • Verify that any information is not conveyed through color alone. Information can be perceived in other ways.
    • Underlining links in addition to coloring them.
    • Add text, shapes, or patterns to lines and bars in charts.
    • Informational labels next to form fields.
    • Additional informative language expressing the importance.
